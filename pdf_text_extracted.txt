Abstract—Food spoilage during transportation is a major chal-
lenge in the supply chain, particularly for perishable fruits and
vegetables, leading to significant economic losses and food safety
concerns. This project proposesFreshSense—a software-driven
predictive analytics framework designed to assess food quality
in real time. The system leverages multi-modal environmental
datasets including temperature, humidity, gas concentration, and
light exposure. Advanced machine learning models, specifically
an ensemble of Random Forest and XG Boost, are applied
to dynamically estimate the remaining shelf life (RSL) and
detect spoilage risk. An Arrhenius-inspired kinetic decay model
runs in parallel as a fail-safe, ensuring uninterrupted shelf-life
estimation. The system generates a Food Quality Score (FQS, 0–
100) and decision-support notifications to enable timely corrective
actions. By integrating predictive modeling with supply chain
logistics, this solution enhances visibility and provides a scalable,
cost-effective approach to reducing post-harvest losses.

I. INTRODUCTION
Food is one of the most fundamental and irreplaceable
resources on Earth, underpinning human health, economic
productivity, and social stability at every level of society.
Yet a significant fraction of all food produced for human
consumption is lost before it ever reaches the consumer.
According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the
United Nations (FAO), approximately1.3 billion tonnesof
food is lost or wasted globally every year—roughly one-third
of total production [1]. A disproportionately large share of this
waste occurs not at the household level butduring transporta-
tion and cold-chain logistics, where perishable commodities
are exposed to uncontrolled environmental conditions that
accelerate microbial and enzymatic spoilage [2].
Traditional supply-chain monitoring systems rely primarily
on static, manufacturer-assigned expiration dates. These labels
offer no mechanism to account for the dynamic temperature
fluctuations and cumulative biochemical changes a product
actually experiences during transit [3]. Even modern mon-
itoring solutions that log environmental parameters remain
fundamentally reactive, often raising alarms only after a safety
threshold has been breached [4].
A further limitation is the historical neglect of chemical
spoilage biomarkers in standard logistics modeling. Perishableproduce undergoes continuous biochemical change: fruits emit
ethyleneas a ripening hormone, while protein-rich foods
releaseammonia (NH 3)andhydrogen sulphide (H 2S)during
degradation [5]. Predictive systems that ignore these gas-
phase signals are effectively blind to the dominant biochem-
ical mechanisms of spoilage. Recent research suggests that
integrating these multi-modal signals with advanced AI can
achieve risk prediction accuracies as high as 97.9% [6].
To address these shortcomings, this paper proposesFresh-
Sense, a comprehensive real-time food quality monitoring and
spoilage prediction framework. The system utilizes a multi-
modal data processing pipeline integrating environmental and
gas-phase variables into a hybrid Machine Learning (ML)
architecture. By combiningRandom ForestandXG Boost
classification with Arrhenius kinetic shelf-life modelling [7],
FreshSense generates a human-readableFood Quality Score
(FQS)that translates complex environmental data into an
actionable metric for logistics operators.
The principal contributions of this work are:
•A predictive data processing pipeline that aggregates
multi-dimensional environmental variables to model food
degradation during transit.
•A hybrid modeling approach that combines the predictive
power ofRandom Forest and XG Boostwith a physics-
grounded Arrhenius kinetic model for precise RSL esti-
mation.
•Development of a Food Quality Score (FQS, 0–100)
that standardizes complex biochemical signals into an
interpretable metric for supply chain stakeholders.
•A robust software architecture capable of identifying
high-risk spoilage scenarios, offering a scalable and cost-
effective solution suitable for diverse logistics environ-
ments [4].

A. Evolution of Cold-Chain and Food Safety Monitoring
Early cold-chain research established the importance of
real-time temperature logging using wireless sensor networks
to mitigate food loss during transportation [10]. Recent ad-
vancements have shifted toward using large-scale databases
for risk assessment. For instance,Sari et al. (2025)utilized
the EU RASFF database to achieve 97.9% accuracy in food
risk prediction using explainable AI [6]. Similarly,Wu et al.
(2025)demonstrated how text mining and machine learning
can identify core risk themes in megacity supply chains, ad-
vocating for dynamic regulatory mechanisms rather than static
"full coverage" models [4]. Furthermore,Jiang et al. (2024)
proposed a fuzzy decision-support framework to improve cost
efficiency and sustainability in supply chain decisions [?].
B. Kinetic Shelf-Life and Mathematical Modelling
The application of the Arrhenius equation to food quality
prediction remains a cornerstone of food science, describing
the temperature-dependence of chemical reaction rates [9].
Srivastava et al. (2023)further advanced this by integrating
mathematical modeling with environmental data to enable real-
time shelf-life estimation [3]. Additionally,Kumar et al.
(2023)demonstrated that continuous temperature monitoring
in cold-chain packaging significantly improves the precision
of these predictive shelf-life models [?].
C. Machine Learning and Ensemble Classification
Machine learning has transformed food quality assessment
into a task of complex pattern recognition. While traditional
models like Support Vector Machines (SVM) have been used
for classification in small datasets [?], newer ensemble meth-
ods have proven more effective. **Random Forest** classifiers
offer high interpretability through feature importance rankings
[8]. More recently, **XG Boost** has emerged as a superior
algorithm for handling imbalanced food safety data, as shown
in the work ofWu et al. (2025), who used it to construct
structured extractions of implicit risk features from high-
dimensional regulatory datasets [4].
D. Chemical Biomarkers and Sensor Technologies
Research into electronic noses and gas sensor arrays has
shown that chemical biomarkers—such as ethylene, NH 3, and
H2S—provide earlier indicators of spoilage than temperature
alone [?].Zia Ul Haq et al. (2023)highlighted that E-nose and
E-tongue technologies paired with machine learning enable
fast, non-destructive detection of contaminants [5]. These
chemical signals, when fused with ambient environmental
data, significantly improve the reliability of early-warning
systems across diverse food categories.
E. Hybrid Predictive Frameworks
The trend in modern research is toward hybrid models that
combine multiple AI techniques.Geng et al. (2023)achieved
extremely high prediction accuracy (R2= 0.998) by using
an optimized LSTM network for food safety risk warning[?]. Similarly,Shehzad (2025)proposed integrating Random
Forest and SVM with deep learning for real-time spoilage
detection within IoT-enabled environments [7].FreshSense
builds upon these works by utilizing a hybrid ensemble of
Random Forest and XG Boost, specifically optimized for
multi-modal environmental data processing without requiring
permanent hardware deployment.
III. METHODOLOGY
The FreshSense system follows a five-phase data-driven
methodology, spanning data acquisition through predictive
alert generation, as detailed below.
A. Phase 1: Data Acquisition and Integration
Unlike traditional hardware-dependent systems, the sensing
layer of FreshSense is designed as asensor-agnostic data
pipeline. It integrates multi-modal environmental variables
that are critical to food stability [5]. The system processes
the following data streams:
•Thermal Dynamics:Ambient temperature readings,
which are the primary drivers of microbial and enzymatic
activity [9].
•Hygroscopic Variables:Relative humidity levels, which
influence moisture loss and surface mold growth in per-
ishables.
•Temporal Tracking:Continuous logging of transit du-
ration to compute cumulative exposure metrics.
•Gas-Phase Biomarkers:Integrated data for CO 2, NH 3,
and Ethylene concentrations, which serve as early-
warning signals for biochemical spoilage [5].
The system is designed to accept structured JSON payloads
from various inputs—including manual telemetry validation
or simulated real-time data—ensuring that the mathematical
shelf-life model (Phase 3A) provides a consistent fail-safe
mechanism regardless of hardware connectivity [3].
B. Phase 2: Data Preprocessing
Raw data is subjected to a preprocessing pipeline to ensure
high-fidelity modeling. Following the best practices identified
byWu et al. (2025), the steps include [4]:
1)Noise Mitigation:A sliding-window median filter is
applied to suppress transient outliers in environmental
time series.
2)Imputation:Forward-fill algorithms handle short-term
data gaps, ensuring continuity in the longitudinal
spoilage profile.
3)Feature Engineering:Three critical derived features
are computed:thermal variation(standard deviation),
volatility(peak-to-peak amplitude), andcumulative ther-
mal exposure(the integral of temperature excess over
time).

E. Phase 5: Decision Support and Risk Mitigation
Following the risk-warning frameworks proposed byGeng
et al. (2023), the system triggers alerts based on three condi-

generates:

routing capacity.

A. System Architecture and Simulation Environment
TheFreshSenseframework is implemented as a cloud-
native predictive service. While the architecture is designed
to interface with edge-computing nodes, the experimental
validation in this study utilizes a high-fidelity simulation en-
vironment. Sensor data—including temperature, humidity, and
gas concentrations—are ingested via a Flask-based REST API.
To validate the system’s robustness across diverse logistics sce-
narios, the input stream utilizes a synthetic dataset simulating
real-time telemetry validation through a dedicated web-based
frontend. This approach follows the validation frameworks for
real-time quality estimation described bySrivastava et al.
(2023)[3].
B. Dataset Characterization
Training and evaluation were conducted on an extensive
dataset of 3,100+ simulated transit records covering41 dis-
tinct food typesacross 9 categories (fresh fruits, vegetables,
leafy greens, dairy, meat, seafood, baked goods, beverages,
and processed foods). Each record contains timestamped envi-
ronmental variables, food-specific optimal storage parameters,
baseline Initial Shelf Life (ISL), and ground-truth labels (Safe,
Warning, Critical). The dataset reflects realistic cold-chain
stressors, incorporating thermal fluctuations and humidity ex-
cursions derived from empirical logistics data distributions.
C. Feature Engineering and Dimensionality
An 18-feature vector was engineered for each record to
capture both instantaneous and cumulative spoilage factors.
Beyond primary inputs, additional features include:
•Log-transformed gas biomarkers:Concentrations of
Ethylene and NH 3were log-transformed (log 1p) to reduce
distributional skewness and improve model convergence,
a technique suggested for fast, non-destructive sensor
detection [5].
•Rolling Statistics:Standard deviation of temperature and
peak-to-peak humidity amplitude to capture environmen-
tal volatility.
•Kinetic Interaction Terms:Integrated products of tem-
perature stress and transit duration based on the Arrhenius
model, providing the non-linear links between time and
thermal decay [3].
D. Training and Optimization Protocol
The ensemble models—**Random Forest (RF)** and
**XG Boost**—were trained using5-fold cross-validationto
ensure generalization and minimize variance. Hyperparameters
were optimized via grid search:
•RF:n_estimators∈ {100,200,500},max_depth
∈ {5,10,15}, andmin_samples_split∈ {2,5,10}.
•XG Boost:learning_rate∈ {0.01,0.1,0.2},
max_depth∈ {3,6,9}, andlambda(L2 regulariza-
tion)∈ {0.1,1,10}.
Following the methodology ofWu et al. (2025), early
stopping rounds were implemented for XG Boost to preventoverfitting on imbalanced risk classes, and tree depth con-
straints were tightened for RF to maintain robustness against
sensor noise [4].
E. Software Stack
The computational backend was developed in Python
3.10 utilizing **scikit-learn** and **XGBoost** libraries
for model development, **Flask** for API orchestration,
and **pandas/NumPy** for data manipulation. The interac-
tive dashboard was built using HTML5, CSS3, and Vanilla
JavaScript, providing real-time visualization of the Food Qual-
ity Score (FQS) and risk-based alert banners.
V. RESULTS ANDANALYSIS
A. Ensemble Model Performance
Following hyperparameter regularization and the removal
of feature leakage, the ensemble architecture achieved ro-
bust predictive accuracy across all three spoilage classes.
The **Random Forest** and **XG Boost** classifiers were
evaluated individually and as a combined ensemble using 5-
fold cross-validation. The ensemble consistently outperformed
the standalone models, achieving a significant reduction in
log-loss and variance, which validates the design choice of
combining bagging and boosting techniques [4].
Key observations from the performance metrics:
•Safe Class:The ensemble achieved high precision (>
96%), indicating reliable identification of non-spoiled
products. This minimizes false-positive alerts, which is
critical for preventing unnecessary food disposal in lo-
gistics operations.
•Warning Class:As expected, the intermediate "Warning"
class exhibited the highest misclassification rate due to
boundary ambiguity. However, the **XG Boost** com-
ponent significantly improved the recall for this class
compared to traditional models, capturing subtle non-
linear shifts in gas concentrations that precede visible
spoilage [7].
•Critical Class:The system prioritized high recall for the
"Critical" class. Genuinely spoiled products were rarely
misclassified as "Safe," mitigating the most consequential
risk in food safety—undiscovered contamination.
B. Food Quality Score (FQS) Validation
The FQS was validated against ground-truth spoilage labels
across the 3,100+ record dataset. FQS values in the 80–
100 range were consistently associated with "Safe" labels,
while values below 50 correctly identified "Critical" records.
The deterministic nature of the FQS formula ensures com-
plete interpretability: logistics operators can audit exactly how
temperature stress or shelf-life consumption contributed to a
specific score, a transparency requirement for explainable AI
frameworks highlighted in recent literature [6].C. Computational Efficiency and Latency
The end-to-end software pipeline—from JSON payload
ingestion at the Flask REST API to dashboard visualiza-
tion—maintained an average latency of under85 ms. This high
throughput confirms the system’s suitability for high-volume
supply chain monitoring. The Arrhenius-based RSL computa-
tion, optimized for rapid execution, completed in under 2 ms,
proving that the mathematical fail-safe can operate effectively
even on low-power secondary computing environments.
D. Shelf-Life Estimation Accuracy
Remaining Shelf Life (RSL) estimates produced by the Ar-
rhenius model were compared against ground-truth values. The
model accurately captured accelerated degradation at elevated
temperatures (5◦Cto40◦C). While the1 + 0.08×T stress
multiplier provided a robust empirical fit, minor systematic
underestimations were noted for specific food categories with
low activation energies. This aligns with findings suggesting
that food-specificE acalibration enhances the precision of
physics-grounded models [3].
VI. EVALUATIONMETRICS
To evaluate the performance of the proposed ensemble
model, standard multi-class classification metrics are em-
ployed. These metrics provide a comprehensive understanding
of the model’s predictive power across the three spoilage risk
classes:Safe,Warning, andCritical.
TheConfusion Matrixserves as the primary tool for
performance analysis, mapping actual class labels against
predicted labels. This allows for the identification of specific
misclassification patterns, such as "Warning" instances being
incorrectly labeled as "Safe."
The performance is quantified using the following metrics:
1)Accuracy:Measures the overall proportion of correctly
classified transit records across all categories.
2)Precision (Macro-Averaged):Measures the average
reliability of the model’s positive predictions across all
classes, ensuring that "Critical" or "Warning" flags are
trustworthy.
3)Recall (Macro-Averaged):Measures the model’s ability
to identify all actual instances of a risk class. In the
context of food safety,recall on the Critical class
is prioritized to ensure that spoiled products are not
erroneously labeled as Safe.
4)F1-Score:The harmonic mean of precision and recall,
calculated as:
F1= 2×Precision×Recall
Precision+Recall(5)
The F1-Score is particularly relevant for this study given
the potential for class imbalance among the 41 food
types.
For multi-class evaluation, we utilizeMacro-Averagingto
ensure that the performance on the "Critical" class is weighted
equally with the more frequent "Safe" class, preventing the
majority class from masking potential safety failures. Precisionon the "Safe" class is also monitored to prevent "false-
spoilage" events, which would lead to unnecessary economic
waste and loss of supply chain trust. Together, these metrics
offer a balanced assessment of theFreshSenseframework’s
readiness for real-world logistics deployment.
VII. CONCLUSION
This study successfully demonstrated the efficacy of the
FreshSenseframework, a hybrid predictive system designed
to enhance food safety and reduce post-harvest losses in cold-
chain logistics. By integrating a physics-grounded Arrhenius
kinetic model with an advanced machine learning ensemble
comprising Random Forest and XG Boost, the system achieves
a robust balance between scientific interpretability and predic-
tive accuracy.
Our experimental validation, conducted across a diverse
dataset of 41 food types, confirms that the ensemble archi-
tecture significantly outperforms standalone models. This is
particularly evident in the identification of early-stateWarn-
ingsignals through non-linear gas biomarker analysis. The
implementation of a sensor-agnostic data pipeline—capable
of maintaining fail-safe shelf-life estimations even during
hardware intermittent connectivity—addresses a critical gap
in current IoT-based supply chain monitoring.
Furthermore, the introduction of the Food Quality Score
(FQS) provides stakeholders with a transparent, actionable
metric that bridges the gap between complex data science
and real-world decision support. Future work will focus on
integrating edge-computing capabilities to reduce latency fur-
ther and exploring deep learning architectures, such as Long
Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks, to better capture the
temporal dependencies of biochemical spoilage. Ultimately,
FreshSenseserves as a scalable blueprint for digitized, data-
driven logistics, contributing to a more resilient and sustainable
global food supply chain.
